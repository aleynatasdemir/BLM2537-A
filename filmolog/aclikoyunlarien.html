Now, let's erase the traces of the books and previous films and go back to what we see and hear in this film alone. The film is set in a post-apocalyptic dystopian geography. After a great war and the subsequent destruction, the civilization as we know it has disappeared or at least its boundaries seem greatly weakened. There is a capital called Capitol where the Panem people live, and there are 12 different regions surrounding it. The Capitol administration and its people live in more than enough prosperity, and as a punishment for a rebellion that rose from the regions years ago, a game similar to gladiator fights is played with children collected from each region, broadcasted on television for the Capitol people to watch. This is called The Hunger Games. We see that the prosperity of the Capitol people, the pressure of their rulers on the regions, and most importantly, The Hunger Games are a reaction. A group called rebels organizes attacks on the city. Although there are very few areas that the Capitol cannot control, the rebels can cause losses to the Capitol people and leaders. While the film's costume designs are quite visually satisfying, the visual effects do not stand out, but they do not turn into a grand cinematic language. Despite focusing on child protagonists as main characters, the dystopian narrative is far more bloody and dark than the audience being defined as children. It contains elements of both a fantasy world like Harry Potter, a dark universe like "1984," and characters that darken as much as Game of Thrones. It immerses the audience into a much more pessimistic and magical world than The Chronicles of Narnia in the midst of a war.
